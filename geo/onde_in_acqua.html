Water Waves

Waves traveling through water don't look much like a sine wave. They are clearly periodic though so, I wonder, how can we model water waves with trigonometry?

In the simple model here, each particle moves in uniform circular motion. They are in sync but the radius of that circular motion decreases exponentially with depth and the phase is proportional to horizontal position. The exact coordinates of the particle moving around the center (x0,y0)(x0,y0) are x(t)=x0+Aeαy0cos(αx0−βt)y(t)=y0+Aeαy0sin(αx0−βt).x(t)=x0+Aeαy0cos⁡(αx0−βt)y(t)=y0+Aeαy0sin⁡(αx0−βt). Note that AA represents the radius of the circular motion at the surface, αα is the reciprocal of the wave length, and ββ is the reciprocal of the period. Horizontal mouse position on this page affects αα, vertical mouse position affects AA, and you can click anywhere on the page to highlight two particles to emphasize their circular motion.
